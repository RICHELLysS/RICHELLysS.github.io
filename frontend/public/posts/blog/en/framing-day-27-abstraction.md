# Abstraction

*Published on January 23, 2021*

I often think about the meaning of abstraction. Let me share my view.

In an era where most "tools" can be replaced by machines, human "imagination" becomes particularly important as what distinguishes us from machines. Now I simply define "imagination" as the appearance of "abstraction," meaning "abstraction" is the essence of "imagination."

Why is abstraction important? My goal is to find a universal, explainable method for learning through imagination. So I ask: what is universal in life? I think it's mathematics, because mathematics is essentially the abstraction of real-world problems.

Speaking of mathematics, the first thing I think of is dimensions. Here's my understanding of dimensions: 0D point, 1D line, 2D surface, 3D solid, 4D time. The relationships: point moves to form line, line moves to form surface, surface moves to form solid, solid moves to form time. This definition focuses only on practicality, not theory. Imagination can abstract anything into four dimensions, so the concept of dimensions can be brought into the learning framework of any discipline, serving to induce.

Note: abstraction does not apply to real-world logic; it depends only on the boundaries of my imagination. It's merely a conceptual tool—as long as it can be used, it has meaning.

Now let's give examples:

Starting with the difficulty of mathematics as I know it: in my mind, 0D corresponds to arithmetic, 1D to equations, 2D to functions, 3D to space vectors, 4D to calculus (description of motion). Going further involves combinations of different dimensions, depending on whether I can imagine them based on the above.

Another angle: language. 0D: characters, 1D: meaning, 2D: combination, 3D: grammar, 4D: expression. This seems clearer.

Another angle: economics (personal framework only). 0D: goods, 1D: transaction, 2D: circulation, 3D: trend, 4D: demand.

Finally, programming: 0D: information, 1D: input/output, 2D: modules, 3D: loops, 4D: functionality.

Thus I can induce everything I learn using this method. This method also helps memory because it's highly analogizable. For example, when learning new knowledge, I substitute the dimensions of programming into the new knowledge, then make some adjustments. When encountering a point in the new knowledge, I can find that point by associating the 1D of programming, forming a thinking channel. This is my method of learning through imagination.

This dimension concept can also be applied to cognition and problem-solving. First, recognize the essence of the problem (0D), then related problems (1D), causes of these problems (2D), how to solve (3D), and post-solution predictions (4D).

Humans are creatures living in four-dimensional space, so perhaps what we can understand and learn is limited to four dimensions (depending on how rich one's imagination is). If I ever become a teacher, I wonder if this method would inspire students (overthinking again).
