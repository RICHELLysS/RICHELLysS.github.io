# Human Plurality

*Published on March 23, 2021*

The concept of "plurality" was proposed by Hannah Arendt based on politics. She believes that what distinguishes humans from animals is the plurality of human thought, based on the highly developed human brain. Here I only simplify this concept into a person possessing two attributes—"group-tending" and "individual-tending"—to integrate into my own system. I feel that many of Arendt's views are one dimension higher than Marx's, but her expression is somewhat obscure, and interpretations cannot reach unity. In fact, this can be directly brought into the model induced in this entry: Marx's thinking favors "strong group connections," while Arendt favors "weak individual connections." One can feel that her thinking has abstractness, which I can better understand and agree with (different thinking modes will be a separate free talk later).

Starting from zero dimension, bringing in the concepts of "variable" and "invariant," human plurality manifests in possessing both "group-tending" and "individual-tending" attributes. The "group-tending" part corresponds to "invariant," the "individual-tending" part to "variable." The essential difference is that "group-tending" is determined by rules, while "individual-tending" is determined by thought. Bring out the Maslow model mentioned in the 28th framing day: the lower two layers (material) are constrained by rules, the upper three layers (spiritual) are constrained by thought.

One dimension raises the question: what is the difference between the two thinking attributes? For ease of understanding, imagine the "group-tending" part as a bubble that does not easily break, and the "individual-tending" part as arbitrarily linkable molecules. In this system, the "group-tending" part follows rules (usually collective consciousness) to integrate into a certain group, forming larger groups and not easily detaching. The "individual-tending" part, based on thought, chooses different thoughts and builds links, not merging with each other and easily disconnecting.

Two dimensions analyze direction. Arendt sees action as interaction between people, and human action is mostly determined by thought. To accomplish something, one must first act. Using the 80/20 rule, thought accounts for 80% of decision-making, action accounts for 20%, but what truly determines outcomes is always action. Then, in the two interaction systems mentioned in one dimension, how does action differ? Arendt identifies three types of human direction: work, labor, and action. Abstracted into my system, work and labor are two modes of action. The essential difference is that labor is determined by rules, work by thought. That is, "labor" belongs to "group" behavior, "work" belongs to "individual" behavior. The relationship between thought and action: thought is subject to external interference before driving action, and the outcome of action reinforces specific thinking patterns. Assuming people can control the "group-tending" and "individual-tending" attributes well, if the benefit of "labor" is less than that of "work," people will favor individual structures; conversely, if greater, they will favor group structures (benefit based on the Maslow model).

At three dimensions, start inducing attributes and abstracting models. The models are divided into "strong group connections" and "weak individual connections." In the 29th framing day, I mentioned the "subject" and "object" attributes of people; bring them into the dimensions of "group" and "individual." Under the "strong group connections" model, the distribution of "subject" and "object" is deterministic; action is formulated by rules; power determines outcomes. Under the "weak individual connections" model, "subject" and "object" are uncertain; action is formulated by thought; ability determines outcomes.

Entering four-dimensional model application: define a group as having at least two people. By observation, one can easily determine a group's interaction pattern. Areas to observe include rule formulation, power distribution, certainty (data), and uncertainty (predicted data). Note that these two models can be nested and combined. For example: surface is strong group connections, subsurface is weak individual connections; temporary strong group connections, long-term weak individual connections; subjective recognition of weak individual connections, objective existence of strong group connections, etc. The best way to distinguish them is to perceive their tendency toward "group" and "individual." This perception process is similar to observing particle motion (though I've never observed particle motion, I can imagine and analogize). Modern society is trending toward atomization. Through observation and thinking, I subjectively believe that people are beginning to move closer to weak individual connections.
