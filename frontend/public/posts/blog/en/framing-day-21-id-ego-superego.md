# Id, Ego, Superego

*Published on December 31, 2020*

First, as shown in Figure 1, human brains from birth have no difference in cognitive potential (generally speaking), possessing input-output, storage, and operation functions. That is, differences in human cognition are entirely determined by environmental influences. This newborn human is in the "id" stage, slowly constructing the "ego," with cognitive abilities unaffected by "class" or "morality." Inequality of class is inequality of information and resource distribution, affecting cognitive potential, leading to different "egos" later.

As shown in Figure 2, in the "id" stage, the most basic human need is "food and clothing," with "reproduction" added as age increases. Linking these to material things, humans are inherently pursuing money and resources—an undeniable fact. No era lacks conflict over money and resources; unequal possession of resources creates capitalism.

Humans aged 0-10 are mostly in the transition from id to ego. During this period, memory is exceptional; rote memorization suffices to "learn" knowledge and pass exams, and they begin forming a strongly self-aware worldview. This worldview may continue through ages 10, 20, 30, 40—some never break through the "ego" bottleneck, believing their way of seeing things is how others see them, some even dying without leaving the ego.

The ego's learning efficiency is low, like a hard drive that can't process new knowledge when full. Ages 10-20 are mostly for ego refinement, and 20-30 for the transition from ego to superego. The difference: those aged 10-20 emphasize "theoreticism," while those 20-30 emphasize "empiricism." The cognitive distinction: words in books are truth vs. books are written by humans.

Regarding "superego," my understanding is that a person finds their value position within a group while recognizing others' positions. A simple example: in the ego stage, after eating a bowl of noodles, one says "I ate a bowl of noodles"; in the superego stage, one asks "How many bowls do you think I ate?" This is a shift from first-person to third-person perspective. The former uses self as mirror, the latter uses others as mirror. Subjective "empathy" ability also improves. Learning is no longer confined to textbooks but discovering commonalities in knowledge and building logical thinking, adapting better to groups, and mastering new knowledge faster—equivalent to the brain successfully constructing a processor (comparable to systems and software).

On this basis, let me chatter about the meaning of learning. The essential meaning of learning is growth. What is the meaning of growth? In fact, the cognitive structure of the human brain has not evolved further; it distinguishes us from animals and plants, but that's it. A modern person is not necessarily smarter than an ancient person. So why learn? For millions of years, human cognition has not changed qualitatively. Learning knowledge does not guarantee more money or higher social status. Erudite people like Newton or Einstein were knowledge pioneers of their time, yet they were not immensely wealthy. In fact, many much dumber people possessed more money and resources. What did these erudite people truly change? The answer: they changed the "tools" humans use (abstract concepts, such as a formula or an idea).

The essential difference between modern and ancient life lies in material life. Ancient people invented spears, tools for hunting in their era; modern people invented guns, also tools for hunting. Human cognitive ability has never progressed; what has progressed is "tools." The essence of learning is to create new "tools" to acquire and allocate resources more efficiently. That is, knot-keeping in ancient times and computer computing in modern times are no different in their respective contexts; the progress of "tools" means the progress of civilization.

Why people can't get into learning is essentially a resource allocation problem. Humans are pleasure-seeking creatures, inherently "lazy." Playing games is pleasurable, watching videos is pleasurable—everything is more pleasurable than studying. Competition in various industries is intensifying, thresholds are rising. After exhausting efforts to cross the threshold, returns fall short of expectations. Truly engaging in a profession doesn't require that much professional knowledge; just knowing how to use "tools" suffices. Skill level doesn't determine how much money one can make, only the quality of the "tools" one creates. Currently, the market is controlled by capital, causing conflict and selling anxiety, profiting from it, valuing traffic over quality. "Learning"—the means to improve tool quality—has been devalued, yet it is an indispensable module for transitioning from ego to superego. Those who believe "learning is useless" fuel the anti-intellectual trend, living entirely within the rules of the ego, willingly exploited by capital.

As shown in Figure 3, Plato's famous cave theory. Prisoners in the cave are bound hand and foot, facing away from the entrance, believing the shadows on the wall are real things, so they crazily pursue shadows. Until one prisoner breaks free, exits the cave, sees the real light, understands the world as it is, and returns to free the others. But after being freed, those prisoners find the outside light too harsh and painful, and begin to hate the one who brought them out.

Fortunately, human cognition never stops growing—only the speed varies. As thresholds rise, more people learn. When more people grow to the superego stage and realize current resource allocation problems, the situation will inevitably change. The black shadows on the wall will fly away like black swans; the prisoners will eventually break their ropes, face the cruel light, drive away gray rhinos, revere knowledge, learn in true wisdom, and create era-specific "tools."

2021 is worth watching.
