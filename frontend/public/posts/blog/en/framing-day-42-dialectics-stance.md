# Dialectical Thinking and Stance

*Published on March 16, 2021*

Recently my thinking entered a strange loop. I found that some already defined concepts can be overturned from another perspective. Arguing with myself eventually led to dialectics in logic.

We probably all know the concept that "everything can be dialectical." Definitions of dialectics vary across philosophical systems. I'll use a common example to analogize: when someone sees a white swan, they might conclude "all swans are white." Using dialectical and critical thinking, we can infinitely "argue" against this: "Not all swans are white because you haven't seen all swans in the world." "How do you know this person would draw that conclusion? You are not them." "You cannot determine that the swan's color you saw is its original color." And so on—endless divergence from new angles before emotions rise.

Letting various dialectical views diverge is very scary (here "scary" is not derogatory). For example, proposing heliocentrism in ancient Rome was resisted by the church, only accepted later, and geocentrism was later ridiculed. But if you jump out of the conventional thinking framework and interpret geocentrism with the spirit of "everything can be dialectical," you'll find that in another reference frame, geocentrism holds (as shown in the figure, from Thinking Lab). Borrowing the concept of relative motion from physics, the common postulate is that the Earth orbits the Sun. But in another reference frame—relative to Earth—the Sun orbits the Earth. Diverging to higher logical directions: as long as the reference frame differs, the definition of things differs. Which reference frame to choose depends only on the number of its supporters and its practicality. From a dialectical perspective, this well explains why anything is possible.

In people's minds, the rightness or wrongness of a thing depends solely on stance. To explain this, I'll analogize with the game Werewolf:

Consider a Werewolf game of the "edge kill" variant (kill all villagers or all special roles). Ten players: three werewolves, four villagers, one seer, one witch, one hunter. At the start, there is an election for the sheriff, who gets 1.5 tie-breaking votes. You draw a werewolf card. How do you win?

In my view, this strategic game is very difficult to win consistently because of its high uncertainty. But by analyzing and simulating different reference frames in your mind to derive stances, each decision becomes clear.

The werewolves' goal is to kill all special roles or all villagers. Assuming varying player skill levels, to ensure your own safety while gaining offensive power, the best way is to pose as the seer and seize the sheriff position to lead the rhythm. To prove you are a real seer, you must simulate the seer's reference frame: normally, relative to werewolves, the seer is a quasi-threat; relative to non-werewolves, the seer is a leader. So in reverse, as a werewolf playing the seer, you and your teammates must create the illusion that you are threatened by werewolves and are leading the non-werewolves. The more you immerse yourself in a reference frame, the more you follow its logic. From that angle, this is the strengthening of stance.

Controlling character stance is very important in a narrative work (personal opinion), whether novels, manga, anime, or film. Stance manifests in how many angles of interpretation are possible. The more complex the stance, the more viewers can interpret from different angles. In some works, a character who kills many people is liked by the audience, while others who kill are universally despised. I think the fundamental reason is the author's control of stance. Each character's stance affects their motivations for decisions, leading to subsequent plot developments.

Example:

Four characters: Princess, Knight, Dragon.

Zero dimension: surface identity labels.

One-dimensional plot (simple drive): Knight defeats dragon, rescues princess, lives happily with her.

Two-dimensional plot (adding purpose): Knight defeats dragon for money, spends time with princess, falls in love, rescues her, and they elope.

Three-dimensional plot (breaking conventions): Knight colludes with dragon to kidnap princess, staging a show just to extort money. The princess learns this, turns the tables, and takes both knight and dragon under her command.

Four-dimensional plot (unique settings change stance): Time loops. In loop one, after being with the princess, the knight tires of her, kills her, and usurps the throne. The princess does not die but enters loop two, wanting to change the outcome. She decides to abandon her identity, elope with the knight to a remote place, and spend her life with him. But the knight, not a good person, offers the princess as dinner to the dragon. The princess enters loop three, knows of the collusion, turns the tables, takes both under her command, and successfully avenges.

From the princess's reference frame, her growth indicates her stance change: from "knight" to "love" to "self." From the knight's reference frame, his stance fluctuates between "fame/fortune" and "love," less clear. Thus in this story, the knight's behavior is less likable than the princess's. If you want to discuss social issues in this story, you could set the knight and princess as enemies of rival nations, swap their genders, set the dragon as the king, add more characters that drive the plot and symbolize groups, etc.—that's another matter.

Discussing these things won't greatly affect the real world. But dialectics helps people build thinking modes, thereby better perceiving the wonderful diversity of the world.
