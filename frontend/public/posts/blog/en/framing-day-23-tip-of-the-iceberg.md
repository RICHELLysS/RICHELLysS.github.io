# Tip of the Iceberg

*Published on January 14, 2021*

Recently I've been learning and perceiving the ability to see through appearances to essence, trying to explain in my own words how differences in essence produce different appearances. I believe correctly inducing this principle can be applied to all aspects of life and learning, achieving mastery through comprehensive understanding.

I'll give two examples. The first is about magic tricks. The same trick performed by an ordinary person vs. a professional magician: the underlying principle is the same, but the magician's performance is far more stunning. Many factors determine the difference: lighting, attention direction, verbal渲染, on-the-spot reaction, etc. The principle itself is the least important. The difference lies in the magician having practiced extensively and genuinely believing in the magic's wonder rather than seeing it as a trick. The essential difference is the level of belief and the time and effort invested driven by that belief.

The second example is about artistic style. I've always believed art is highly subjective because different aesthetics yield different "beauty." However, there are objective factors in art, as art often stems from receiving and processing information from life. Children are still naive about the world; they draw the "shape" of an object based on brief perception but don't know its definition. For example, drawing an ear: a beginner draws a circle based on impression, unable to perceive the ear's "structure" (excluding geniuses), still in a chaotic stage (as shown in the figure—I like nested rings to represent chaos), following only "intuition." After entering the door of painting, through copying, sketching, and other practice, they figure out the "structure" of objects, pursuing "likeness." Later, after building a set of cognitive "structure" for objects, they enter a stage of exploring personal style, eventually returning to pursuing "intuition." Picasso is an artist with a distinct personal style. His early works were "normal" to the public, but later his style became increasingly strange, departing from popular taste into so-called "childlike drawings," yet highly visually impactful and memorable, becoming representative of abstract painting.

From these two examples, we learn that for two phenomena identical in "shape," what people see is only the tip of the iceberg. It's like the eyes saying "Ah, I get it" but the hands saying "You don't." Anything without polishing and deliberate practice (broadly including crafts, acting, design, writing, and other human creations) presents a "shape" lacking spirit—bluntly, without a soul. "Shape" can be quickly learned and imitated, but it melts away quickly, failing the test of time and depreciating rapidly. In contrast, the "essence" honed through deliberate practice has lasting value; experienced eyes recognize it as extraordinary. What people see is only the tip of the iceberg, and they judge others based solely on that tip. If someone seems impressive and remains impressive after some time, they are far more excellent than you see, having invested more time and effort than you imagine—such people have long-term value. Conversely, if someone who seemed impressive no longer feels impressive after a while, they only had a well-crafted "shape" and lack long-term value.

"Shape" can blind the eyes. Chasing "shape" for its own sake is meaningless. In a world full of "shape," it depreciates quickly, and people will come to appreciate truly valuable things. If everyone realizes the "tip of the iceberg" principle, they can view the essence of phenomena more rationally, raise their internal value standards, and develop reverence for "essence."
