# Crow and Designer

*Published on September 10, 2021*

A crow landed on the designer's windowsill.

"Long time no see, Brainhole Crow," the designer turned his head.

"Long time no see," the crow opened its wings in salute.

"What trouble have you encountered? That's probably why you're here." The designer extended his left hand for the crow to perch on.

"Perhaps my belief has deviated, making it impossible for me to connect with reality. This troubles me greatly." The crow's round eyes turned into semicircles.

"That's normal. Deviation always exists, doesn't it?"

"But I've gradually lost my sense of commonality with people—in your words, the ability to empathize and rationally understand. It can't be quantified, and it's hard to analogize accurately."

The designer thought for a moment, then replied: "It's like this. When A communicates with B, there are six facets: A's view of B, B's view of A; A's view of B's view of B, B's view of A's view of A; A's expectation of B's view of A, B's expectation of A's view of B. These six facets are stored in the human brain as six images. The repetitiveness of the image characteristics determines the level of ability to sense commonality. For example, in my eyes you are a crow, in your eyes I am a designer. I think your view of yourself is a crow, you think my view of myself is human. I expect your view of me to be a friendly designer, you expect my view of you to be a smart crow. If the images I described above are not much different from what you have in mind, then our commonality is very high."

"I understand!" The crow flapped its wings. "But wouldn't the same appearance create more commonality?"

"Individuals with certain common characteristics attract each other and form groups—that's a known framework. Actually, the reason you came here to ask me..." The designer paused. "Is to give you a human appearance, right?"

"But I don't want to become human." The crow narrowed its eyes. "My eyes are made of brainholes. You know, if I were transformed into a human, I wouldn't be able to grow normally."

As if anticipating this, the designer opened the diamond grid space. "The organization has developed a biological machine with all the traits of humans." He stepped into the space. "The only difference..."

Before them stood a large number of identical-faced "humans," neatly lined up on biological charging plates.

"They don't have the advanced operating system of the human brain."

"What does that prove?"

"You can live in their brains and become their operating system." The designer pressed a button, and a row of machines moved forward on their base plates.

"It's in the third testing phase. I can give you one—just in time to help collect practical data."

The crow scanned the humanoid machines, its gaze stopping on one. "Why are the shoulders asymmetrical?"

"LYSS, my first-edition design, completed in a half-asleep, half-awake state. Normally, a machine with such an obvious flaw would be eliminated in the first round of testing. Indeed, because of the asymmetrical design, its numerical fluctuation is largest, indicating the worst stability. But unexpectedly... test results show its average skill score and total score are slightly higher than the other machines."

The crow stared into LYSS's hollow eyes—their contours were also different sizes.

[Mr. Crow, my tail has become a shell.]

The crow remembered the day it met a cat with strange bone structure and odd personality, unable to jump flexibly like other felines.

"Sometimes I wonder why my eyes and head are different from other crows..."

The designer understood and smiled gently.

"LYSS has found its operator."
