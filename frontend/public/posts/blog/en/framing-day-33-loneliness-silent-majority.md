# Loneliness and the Silent Majority

*Published on February 16, 2021*

This entry is a free talk, subjectively expressing some views on groups and individuals.

I am not sociable, nor am I good at socializing. The reason traces back to elementary school. Due to some psychological issues, I went a whole month without speaking to anyone. That period probably created a gap between me and the group. Some may not understand what it's like to be isolated from a group; to them, it might be very painful. But from another perspective, for people like me, the pain of being in a group is equivalent to their pain of being alone.

"Humans are social animals." I disagree with this statement because only social people think humans are social animals. Often, when you refute a group, you lose because no one around supports your view. So in fact, those who are not suited to group living choose silence.

Most people are silent most of the time, not easily expressing their views—that's my opinion. Everyone's views differ, yet everyone wants their views to be supported by the group. The advantage of a group is that the side with more supporters often "wins," while the "losers" remain silent. There is no absolute "right" or "wrong" in this world; it's only about how many people support a view. When a view gains a certain number of supporters, it becomes a "rule."

When I'm alone, I often think: how can I convey my meaning precisely and without causing controversy? After enjoying solitude, my greatest gain is the power of thinking. I simulate scenes in my mind, predict outcomes. In the world I create, I am the god who can control everything; anything can happen, depending on the size of my brainhole. But when in a group, it's not easy to express opinions, and people's actions are not predictable. Your opinions will be gazed at from all directions, generating different interpretations. You begin to fear, fear expressing opinions, because information asymmetry is fatal. Interpretation of anything depends on the time and space point from which it is viewed—completely unpredictable. When I used to do reading comprehension, I would simulate a scene in my mind, place a person in it, and answer based on my prediction of that person's behavior and consciousness. It all depended on whether my prediction was correct. Sometimes I could see the character from many angles and draw different conclusions, but I didn't know which was the correct answer (perhaps why I'm relatively better at math).

I feel a tear with groups because groups generate too many possibilities; I can't predict them. When I think about how to rationally judge and use my actions to control the direction, my speech becomes restricted. And when I recall that a past scene or remark could be interpreted differently by others, I become extremely afraid to speak. In my world, anything is possible, everything has a reason, everything that exists has its rationale—even a screw could cause huge changes, depending on the probability. That's why I'm obsessed with the butterfly effect. Of course, I also know how to refute this statement: "The sun rises from the west, apples float upward"—is that possible? But I can still refute: "East, west, south, north are defined by humans. If you redefine the original east as west, then the sun rises from the west. Or you can create a mirror world through science fiction or other media, refining language precision: in this world, the sun rises from the west." The same applies to apples—it all depends on your imagination.

Isn't this absurd? I can imagine countless refutations, escalating to questioning my entire thinking mode, or attacking the meaning of the question itself. These predictions spin in my head, ultimately making me not dare to say a word. This is an example of me crushing myself, and also how I concluded through observing groups that "my views are unlikely to be accepted by the majority."

So lonely people willingly seek answers in books, refine their own rules, find statements that support their views, eliminate ambiguity in expression, and think about how to gain group support. This is very contradictory. In this process, people confront chaos. Accustomed to solitude, they don't know what role to play in a group, nor how their expressed emotions will be interpreted by others—like quantum collapse: when you close your eyes, quanta move freely; when you open them, they collapse into a fixed state.

When I see arguments happening, I clearly know the possible outcomes. I also know what choices I would make in that situation. But most of the time, I choose silence. Individuals are influenced by groups, and groups are composed of individuals. In fact, most people choose silence. They gaze at the same thing from different angles and do not easily express opinions. They only desire to gain more support when they do express them. If they don't gain support, they generate various negative emotions and deny themselves or others to varying degrees. This, in my view, is human commonality. Loneliness seeks support to refine rules; the silent are often the majority.

Thus, society, composed of groups, is truly profound. Modern society has become atomized. Perhaps new kinds of groups will emerge. I am optimistic about this.
