# Star Chasing

*Published on April 21, 2020*

Can't sleep in the middle of the night, so I'll write some random thoughts.

On star chasing:

In middle school, I was a standard otaku and didn't understand the 3D culture of star chasing. Turns out, you can't escape the law of "true fragrance." I've been a fan of a boy band for nearly two years—the longest I've stayed in any fandom (I used to lose interest after half a month). And I like them more and more. This might be the only boy band I'll ever stan.

Fandoms always have frustrating things happen. Even very chill circles can't avoid being attacked. Recently, something very confusing happened: fans in my circle were inexplicably cyberbullied. Rival toxic fans spread rumors, slandered our idols, and tried every way to dig up dirt—even making up fake scandals.

This is the most disharmonious event I've experienced since joining the fandom.

I lost sleep for days, desperately clarifying and cleaning up the search terms. The previously peaceful, low-key circle was filled with negative words. It was unbearable. I couldn't imagine what the inner world of those who wrote such words is like. While insomniac, I kept thinking about the causes and effects of these confusing events. The more I thought, the more my head hurt. Are those people chasing stars or chasing a sense of existence within the fandom?

I subjectively divide the purposes of star chasing into four types:

1. Those who look at idols' looks to satisfy their own desires, projecting fantasies onto idols. Easy to understand—that's what personas are for. Mommy fans, girlfriend fans, little puppy or energetic girl types. Very common. Some find it distasteful, but it's a fact. They make up a large portion of consumers. Many just look at faces for fun and don't have the energy to care about much else.

2. Those who value the work and consume for the idols' work. Also quite common. Looks may be a plus, but they focus more on musical empathy, stage presence, etc. They have a high chance of becoming long-term loyal fans.

3. Those who become fandom leaders, seeking a sense of existence they can't get in real life. These are typical fandom chasers. They may have started just following idols, but gradually found a sense of existence online and feel powerful. There are very positive examples—becoming a big fan and driving more people to vote, organize events, and fundraise. And very negative examples—trolls and those who stir up drama fall into this category.

4. Those who use idols as a mood regulator, motivation to move forward, wanting to improve together with their idols. These are the core fans of a circle, the most benign type. They continuously draw strength from idols while balancing real life. The only downside is that if the idol's persona collapses, they need to find the next idol.

After writing this, I finally feel a bit sleepy. Recently, the motivation to move forward has indeed come from star chasing, and the negative emotions also come from the fandom—a double-edged sword. This event taught me more about fandoms. Unexpectedly, trouble really can come out of nowhere. Not everyone can maintain an objective attitude to sort through an incident. Following the crowd to hate is scarier than hating for a reason. The internet is a magnifying glass. The wider it spreads, the more it magnifies human nature and group effects. I can't summarize all these effects yet. To conclude: the greatest benefit of star chasing is gaining motivation. Anything contrary to that goes against self-interest. Think about it: trolls and haters waste their precious energy filling their brains with negative words—how "altruistic" of them.
