# Redefining Family

*Published on July 26, 2021*

How to define the concept of "family"? From a sociological perspective, a family is actually a small social relationship system. Individuals form a "family" due to shared interests, distributing resources according to a certain mechanism. Here, interests include not only money but also assets, spiritual support, knowledge, and a series of concrete or abstract things corresponding to individual needs.

Can blood relationships between individuals be a key factor in forming a family? In the Chinese legal system, individuals with blood relationships automatically assume responsibilities assigned by family identity, which cannot be severed. But from the perspective of my ideal society, blood ties should not be a key factor. The fundamental reason, I think, is that the concept of "blood" itself carries "power." "Power" alienates human thought—constantly satisfying the desire to control while raising the threshold of status perception. "Power" in society causes various inequalities, let alone within a family. The key factor should start from individual needs. If two individuals can achieve a long-term, comfortable balance of supply and demand under a defined system, then they can form a family, like two puzzle pieces fitting together.

How to define "discomfort"? For me, "comfort" is tied to drawing boundaries. Simply put, if an individual cannot draw boundaries according to their own will when facing things, it's very hard to subjectively distinguish "comfort" from "discomfort." If a person is in a family with a poor concept of drawing boundaries, they are often kidnapped by the "identity" assigned by the family. Classic sentence patterns: "You are xx, I am xx, so you should..." Or using very strong subjective consciousness to attack the weaker party's subjective consciousness. In severe cases, this forms a deformed relationship of oppressor and oppressed symbiosis. In such relationships, "comfort" also becomes deformed. So to build a good family concept, there must be a clear definition of boundaries between people, both materially and spiritually, so that people can truly enjoy the various positive values a family brings.

Communication is very important and irreplaceable for forming a family. This is a conclusion I reached after lengthy, fruitless communication with my original family. When handling relationships in society, people have two spiritual abilities: empathy and rational understanding. Zooming into a family, the party with greater ability assists and educates the party with lesser ability in these two abilities. The means is "communication." A person in a family that can neither empathize nor rationally understand and refuses to communicate, even if they receive sufficient material goods, will suffer huge spiritual damage. How big? Can't define, but analogizing: it should be like two completely dissonant audio tracks overlaped together. If more than two, even harder to overlay.

How to rationally detach from one's original family, form a family with someone unrelated by blood, and not be morally kidnapped by the original family? Here's a solution: return to legal provisions. First, a person's identity and relationships in a "family" cannot be detached or severed. This means "I" must assume my responsibilities in the original family. So I need to draw my own boundaries through "decluttering," while also allocating some resources to the original family. From the current situation, I occupy most of the family's financial resources, while suffering great spiritual harm and satisfying some of my family's need for control. Suppose at some future point I achieve financial independence and form a "family" under my defined system (or even form a family as a single person), I should give back most of the financial resources to the original family, while rejecting any form of spiritual contribution or satisfaction of their control desires. For "me," an unbalanced social system would thus relatively return to balance. Whether this view satisfies most individuals and groups is not up for discussion (after all, studying groups from a social perspective is really complex).
