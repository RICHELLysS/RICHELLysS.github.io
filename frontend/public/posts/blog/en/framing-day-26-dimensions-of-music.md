# Dimensions of Music

*Published on January 21, 2021*

I have my own perspective on music in my mind. Now I attempt to explore and define it as a rule for evaluating music.

First, what is music and what is its meaning? Music is a series of continuous vibrations at specific frequencies combined into harmonious sounds that evoke emotional resonance. I believe the greatest meaning of music is to express emotions. Humans have created many words to describe different emotions—happiness, sadness, anger, excitement, etc.—to make others understand. But words are finite; mixed emotions are hard to express. That's when music is needed.

In my view, good music, without restricting to a particular style, must satisfy three points: interesting to listen to, harmonious, and immersive.

Good music necessarily requires four dimensions to complement each other to meet the above three points:

Threshold comfort, rhythmic drive (groove), narrative, atmosphere.

These four elements correspond to the four main instruments of a band: bass, drums, piano, and guitar.

Abstracting the four instruments for substitutability: bass sound, rhythm, main melody, and secondary melody. The main melody is usually vocals, but I prefer instrumental music, so it becomes an instrument with a particularly prominent timbre. Simply put, comparing the four components to a bowl of noodles: bass = bowl, rhythm = broth, main melody = noodles, secondary melody = toppings. Missing any one makes it an incomplete bowl of noodles.

With these definitions, we can analyze specific music. Any combination of the four components can express different emotions, varying the four dimensions, thus producing different styles.

Examples:

1. Rock

Typically, rock highlights rhythm and secondary melody, giving strong rhythmic drive and atmosphere, with relatively weaker narrative. The main melody must be strong enough to stand above the rhythm and secondary melody to be called a song; otherwise, it becomes all accompaniment. That's why rock is relatively balanced (meaning not favoring any dimension). How to describe rock? I think rock is very exhilarating, like a helicopter that instantly lifts emotions. The singer is at a high emotional point, and the listener also releases emotions—very stress-relieving.

2. Symphony

Using an instrument like the violin (sharp and tough) as the main melody, the main and secondary melodies often call and respond, with the next voice entering before the previous fades. Strong narrative; with rich imagination, you can play a movie in your mind. You can also easily hear the composer's personality and mood. I once listened to Beethoven's Symphony No. 5 while walking, and in a few minutes, I experienced a person's tragic life.

3. EDM

A very popular modern style. Its highlight is the progressive rhythm, strong drive. The bass often merges with the rhythm. The secondary melody alternates with the main melody, sometimes overpowering it. I have mixed feelings about EDM. If the main and secondary melodies aren't well controlled, it easily lacks identity. I feel this style is frivolous (not derogatory); if not controlled, it loses heaviness, giving a fast-food feel. But it always has a strong sense of technology and a feeling of "in progress" (odd description), casting a fantasy glaze over restless cities.

(More examples can be analogized similarly.)

The Shape of Music

This is an abstract concept referring to the shape in time, depending on how the musician hones their music. When I hear a song, a shape appears in my mind: is it a work with broad appeal but not lasting long (flat shape), or narrow appeal but loved deeply by a few for a long time (long shape)? This depends on the four dimensions; higher dimensions create more resonance.

Why is Jay Chou considered the ceiling of Chinese pop music? Even without lyrics, just listening to the accompaniment, you can hear that the secondary melody consists of more than one instrument, creating an atmosphere worth savoring. His voice is light and soft; as the main melody, it doesn't compete with the secondary melody but skillfully combines with it, singing with the atmosphere in narrative. It's elegant like a symphony yet retains popular charm. His music achieves the balance of appealing to both refined and popular tastes—no one else does this.

How to define refined (ya) and popular (su)? I think the premise of "ya" is distance: a sense of distance is "ya"; being too close is "su." This relates to spatial sense in music. Dimensions have levels and contrasts, following the principle of neither too far nor too close, with harmony as the core. Why is some music harsh? Because the foreground is too close, especially a particularly prominent instrument stealing the spotlight from later sounds, violating harmony. The main melody singer shows off their voice instead of fitting into the accompaniment to create spatial sense. It's novel the first time, but tires on repeated listening. In other words, inharmonious foreground treatment flattens the shape of music.

This exploration is somewhat abstract, but music itself is abstract, as abstract as emotions. The more abstract something is, the more I can string it together with imagination. Perhaps one day I can abstract the things in my mind into music, and listeners will know what my soul looks like.
