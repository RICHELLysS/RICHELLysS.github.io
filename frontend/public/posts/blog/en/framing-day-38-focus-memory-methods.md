# Focus and Memory Methods

*Published on March 8, 2021*

I've experienced this: during a lecture, I get sleepy or distracted halfway through. How to avoid it? I usually use this method:

Try to notice the numbers the speaker says and remember their order, then use numerical intuition to retrieve information from your mind. For example, in a statistics course, the lecturer mentions 10%, 50%, 7, and 4. When recalling, ask yourself: what relates to 10%, 50%, 7, and 4? At first, it's hard to recall. At that point, note the number order in your notebook, then give analogical meaning and abstract and diverge in your mind.

For example, diverge from the number 5: five fingers, pentagram, pentagon, the number 5 on a calculator, the 5 on a keyboard, "knowing destiny at fifty," the number 5 in the Eight Trigrams, five percent, the character "Fifth Person," the fifth number appearing in class, "Assassin 567," hook... (got distracted).

If before zoning out, you recall knowledge points related to specific numbers from class, the link is built, and you can naturally answer questions. If not, too bad—lose points and rely on metaphysics (?) This method works for short-term memory.

Given that I am very superstitious about numerology, I give different numbers different meanings to enhance impression. I often use number combinations to divine in my mind. For example, waking up and checking my phone, the time is 7:34. 7 is a powerful and popular number (my personal definition). 3+4=7, a coincidence. 34 is twice 17, and 17 is a combination I like. In Qimen Dunjia, 3 and 4 correspond to "wood growth." So I judge that today is suitable for happy learning and my brain will work well. When I see numbers somewhere, I can't help but remember them. The drawback is that I remember certain unimportant things very clearly, easily getting distracted. But relatively speaking, it's still helpful for training the brain.

This habit may make one slightly obsessive-compulsive about numbers. An example: when arranging photos of aquatic life taken at Sanya Aquarium, I arrange elements in a certain numerical order: two ducks, one rabbit, one alpaca, one parrot, two parrots (connected end to end); two fish, three starfish, four jellyfish, many worm-like creatures, countless creatures (increasing order), etc. Forcing numerical elements into a sequence like a child who just learned to count "one apple, two apples." But I find it very interesting because it gives numbers life. An even more interesting case: playing Sudoku, I sometimes mutter to myself, "4, where have you gone? 8, don't flash. 1, come out and plant a flag." Over time, because of numbers, I feel less lonely, and the world gains magic. So when my attention and memory are good, it's entirely thanks to this strange memory method of giving meaning to numbers.
