# The Ninth Art

*Published on February 4, 2021*

Define art as the transmission of spirit. Literature is linear transmission of spirit, painting is visual transmission, music is auditory transmission, dance is bodily transmission, theater is conflict transmission, film is audiovisual transmission, sculpture is three-dimensional transmission, architecture is concrete transmission. The above is an overview of the traditional eight art forms. So what is commonly considered the ninth art?

Before discussing the ninth art, let me introduce an experiment. American psychologist B.F. Skinner placed a mouse in a special box with a button. When the mouse pressed the button, food was dispensed. Over time, the mouse formed an association between pressing and receiving food. If pressing didn't yield food, the mouse pressed even more frequently. This is a simple game rule: perform a task, receive a reward. Human ancestors were rodent mammals. After the dinosaurs' extinction, they survived in caves due to their small size. Evolutionarily, humans are in some ways very similar to that mouse. You can think of games as a giant Skinner box, where people press keyboard keys or touch screen positions at different frequencies and receive visual rewards. You know it's just data, but you still enjoy it.

I often think: what is the meaning of spirit? If humans are dopamine-seeking creatures, injecting dopamine directly would provide instant pleasure. So why are there so many different ways to obtain dopamine? The answer I arrived at is that outcomes don't produce dopamine; the process does. When you open your inventory and see that pile of data, you recall the actions you took to acquire it and the pride you felt, and you can't help but perform the next action. In this process, you develop an obsession, a belief, gradually coalescing into a spirit. That is, dopamine rewards derived from spirit drive human action and development.

Returning to art, with the above groundwork, you've probably guessed: video games are the modern ninth art. The ninth art's spiritual forms are highly diverse, combining literature, music, theater, dance, film, architecture, sculpture, and painting. The creators combine the first eight arts to construct a Skinner box, then place humans inside—that's the ninth art. What distinguishes the ninth art from traditional arts is that while transmitting spirit, it also creates spirit. The essential reason is interactivity. When you immerse yourself in the ninth art and interact with it, an inseparable spiritual link forms.

But without reward mechanisms, would anyone truly experience the spirit within? This art form can be a social method, a money-making method, or a venting method. Yet only when you truly gaze upon and feel the art can you perceive its beauty. Gradually, you become aware that the creator lives on in another way—spiritually. Ordinary people have a bias against pure artists: they think artists can't make money in their lifetimes, only becoming famous after death, and they criticize their works. This is a materialistic, institutionalized mindset. People fear being different, so they willingly become part of the group. Sun Wukong in Journey to the West reflects this phenomenon: from rebellious and arrogant to seeking help from various gods, finally becoming one among many Buddhas—this reflects the process of being institutionalized. Artists are usually different. They have strong self-awareness and spiritual direction. Some compromise with the group, but more often, to avoid institutionalization, they choose to live in another way.

Take Van Gogh: such a talented painter, short-lived, didn't earn enough money in his lifetime. Everyone mourns his death, but I don't see it that way. I think his death allowed him to live on in this world—in another way. Wherever you see his paintings, whenever people discuss him or write about him, you can't help but marvel: his spirit has spread for a long time. That's proof that he's still alive. Isn't that the meaning of art? When a person creates a work that conveys a unique, unreplicable spirit, regardless of whether it can be exchanged for money, the vitality that continues is priceless. This may not be understood by most, may be ridiculed by the extremely institutionalized, or interpreted as nihilism. But as time passes, some people truly calm down to perceive it, see similar landscapes, and find meaning in life and death, also creating a mirror to reflect their inner selves.

Every art form is a carrier of spiritual civilization. The ninth art is the carrier of modern spiritual civilization. Only by transcending the instinct to seek rewards can one understand the true spiritual value behind the ninth art—the scenes constructed from data: flowers, trees, buildings, vivid characters, moving stories, different paths and choices, spreading upward and deeper—the creator's spirit world, romantic to the end.
