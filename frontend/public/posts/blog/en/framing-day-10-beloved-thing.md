# Beloved Thing

*Published on February 22, 2020*

After a long time, I picked up a pencil to sketch again, and suddenly many emotions surged up, making my nose tingle. If I plan to live to fifty, I will spend the first thirty years earning enough money to live comfortably, and the last twenty years fully devoted to painting. Even if my relatives blame me for my shortsightedness and no one appreciates my work, at least I won't be homeless. I can't describe the feeling—sketching with a pencil again, adding shadows bit by bit, suddenly I felt alive. To define it idealistically: suppose a person's soul is bound to one thing in their lifetime, and they have a "spiritual love" for that thing. For some it's science, for others music, wealth, or even another person. This gives them endless energy when doing related work. In childhood, one's spirit is inexhaustible, but as we grow, the spiritual circle gradually shrinks until it can only hold one or two things. Then a person only resonates with those one or two things—or unluckily, none at all. At this point, personality differentiation is basically complete. You hear different opinions: "XXX is so hard." "I don't think so." "I really love XXX." Further subdivision: some love something but aren't good at it—classic example: loving to sing but being tone-deaf. Without sample analysis, we don't know how many people love something but lack the ability to pursue it. Others love something and are good at it, but external factors prevent them from doing it—classic example: a father wants his son to take over the family business, but the son can't appreciate his "good intentions." Then there are those who don't love something but are good at it—we won't discuss that here. With that premise, I can start expressing: today I sketched again. Mechanical pencil doesn't feel as good as wooden pencil. My shading improved slightly, but facial expressions still lack something. Two hours passed without boredom. Then I remembered I hadn't touched my schoolwork, and a wave of frustration hit me. Only then did I realize where my soul lies. Now, the only way forward is to keep going. I know I'm not as hardworking or intelligent as others. The road ahead is foggy. Escaping into virtual worlds has become boring. Then I dreamed again of where my soul used to return, touched my old spiritual support. After much thought, it was like waking from a dream. The fog slowly cleared, and looking ahead, there were roads everywhere—just overgrown with weeds.
