# Engineering and Art

*Published on January 9, 2021*

Recently I've realized that I am a daydreamer, not creating any practical value. Yet without daydreaming, life feels too dull, spirit lacks pursuit, and I can't focus on books—so I start daydreaming again, in a cycle. In this entry, I finally begin to structure my values.

## The Value of Engineering

Here I superficially define engineering as building applications that efficiently process materials and energy to meet human needs. The value of engineering lies in materiality. The more practical and faster to produce an engineering product, the higher its value, reflected in efficient material management and resource allocation (so much theoretical definition is useless for a daydreamer). That is, without capital interfering in product packaging, if you're an engineer wanting to make money and keep a good reputation, you need to design or build something practical and mass-producible—relatively objective.

## The Value of Art

In my view, the value of art lies in spiritual transmission. Artworks focus on conveying the abstract thing called "beauty." Since everyone's aesthetics differ, art is essentially subjective. Artworks that survive to this day must have "beauty" that continues to resonate and be appreciated, along with their cultural value.

With no foundational value framework yet, I can only chatter randomly. How to combine engineering and art? Take buying a pair of shoes: most people first notice whether they look good, using "good-looking" or "not" to gauge price. Engineers (including shoe designers) care about how long the shoes last and how comfortable they are to walk in. This creates a divergence: engineers might sacrifice aesthetics for comfort, leading to conflicts with art designers (only to find capitalists win—how off-track). The typical example is the Mac computer—a product that finds a balance (though imperfect) between engineering and art. It's thin, light, and its shape fits minimalist beauty perfectly, interpreting how obsessive Steve Jobs was. Its flaws are obvious: quick battery drain, poor heat dissipation, hard-to-remove internal parts, incompatibility with other hardware (capitalists say this sells more products—great).

Such a product is already a successful combination of engineering and art, satisfying visual aesthetics while being functionally competitive. Similar examples abound. In electronics, final products all pursue visual beauty. With similar performance, "appearance" is increasingly competitive in the market (humans are visual creatures—who doesn't like beautiful things? Painful realization). So, capturing attention with appearance first, then improving reputation and quality, is indeed a clever marketing strategy. Got it.

The balance between engineering and art is ultimately a trade-off between "beauty" and "practicality." Achieving both requires more learning, thinking, and experience accumulation. More daydreaming seems meaningless. For now, I'll note down the questions and slowly seek answers along the way.
