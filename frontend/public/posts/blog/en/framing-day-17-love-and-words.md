# Love and Words

*Published on September 30, 2020*

I've wanted to write about my view of love for a long time, but it's hard to grasp, so I put it off until after I moved to the UK and had fewer burdens. It's not really a serious discussion about love, just an analysis and examination of my own view, and my subjective thoughts on words.

I read an article saying that a person's various views are basically formed by age twenty. At nineteen, I think my view of love should be stable—I hope I'm not wrong.

Actually, my enlightenment about love came from shipping fictional couples. The earliest was in elementary school, shipping March and April from "Stealing Star September Sky." I cried uncontrollably when reading that tragic manga. That couple was strong against strong, hurting and saving each other. That set my preference for double-strong couples. Later, I jumped between fandoms. In middle school, I stayed long in an anime called "K"—the red and blue kings, Mikoto and Munakata, their love-hate relationship made me addicted. Immersed in fanfics and fanarts on Tieba, I believed for a while that love should be like that, genderless. In high school, I jumped through several more fandoms. One year, I was obsessed with "The King's Avatar," falling for the Zhou Ye pairing, another double-strong couple. I have to mention that the quality of fanfics in The King's Avatar fandom is extremely high—more exciting than some novels I've read and rarely left unfinished. The audience includes both male and female readers, with various settings and genres. It was the initial reason I abandoned original writing for fanfiction. Whenever I hit a block, revisiting that fandom always helps me find my flow.

So what's the point of all this? You're talking about your hobby of shipping, not your own view of love? Good question. The problem is, I've never truly understood how two people interact in real life. I'm even confused by the twists and turns of some real-life romantic relationships. This means my view of love is disconnected from the 3D world, very abstract. The result: relationship experience: 0, social skills: 0.

What is my view of love? Influenced by the couples I've shipped, it's simple to summarize, a phrase seen in many places: "Together, unrivaled; apart, each a king." That might not describe love, because love should involve dependence. I think two people's personalities are like two semicircles with bumps and indentations. Complementarity makes a truly compatible couple. But when a circle is already complete, needing no complementary parts, the only thing it can get along with is another complete circle. Sometimes circles resonate, attract each other, but also repel, maintaining a subtle distance. When too lonely, they stick together; when needing solitude, they quickly separate. That's my view of love. I personally dislike depending on others and also dislike others depending on me (to put it bluntly, I'm asocial). I only like weak ties, not strong ones (low-carbon interpersonal interaction). I think when someone is feeling down, you give them a little strength, and when I'm down, I get some strength from them. Over time, if it becomes a fixed cycle of strength supply, that forms love. I know many people find this view of love strange and disagree.

How did I realize my view of love is like this? Thanks to writing fanfiction. People often say shipping isn't about the original relationship but the imagination derived from it. Indeed, in different fanfics by different authors, the same couple takes on different images. There are a thousand Hamlets in a thousand people's eyes. Words are mirrors, reflecting people's desires and fantasies. When I write fanfiction, controlling the characters' directions, the number of likes varies. With comparison, after reading other authors' works and then my own on the same couple, I suddenly realize how I see the two characters. Words have emotions. When I make the two protagonists fall in love, I unconsciously insert my own views, and a low number of likes means few readers resonate.

I remember a brilliant author in one of my fandoms who wrote a story set in an alternate world where people awaken a spiritual platform called the Realm of Sympathetic Resonance at a certain age. On this platform, you can see how many people resonate with your realm and choose to become soulmates with them. Some never see anyone and die alone; some find their soulmate upon awakening. The male protagonist's platform always had only himself until one day he saw a figure in the distance, very far away, like a star. He spent his life searching for that person. Meanwhile, the other person's platform also had only that one star, also spending a lifetime seeking his figure. They only met in old age. This story greatly inspired me. Many thought it was heartbreaking, with pain in the middle and sweetness only at the end. But as an old shipper, I think it's the most romantic love I've ever seen. Time is the most terrible, merciless poison and blade. To believe, within finite time, that you will meet someone who resonates with your spiritual world, and to never extinguish that obsession even as you age into the grave—to never compromise from birth to death—in my opinion, that is realizing the greatest value of life. The existence of this belief in love is the highest, purest romanticism. The greatest value and purpose of words is inheritance: to write down various spirits and beliefs in different story forms, so that more people resonate, believe, and find meaning in living in this world. Reading a text that resonates is immense happiness.
