# Overcoming Aesthetic Bias

*Published on April 5, 2021*

First, capture my own bias against my appearance:

"I am a very ugly person. So ugly people must do better in other areas to gain others' recognition. Ugly people don't need to dress up, because they're still ugly after dressing up—like a negative number plus a positive number that still doesn't reach positive. That person is beautiful, handsome, so they gain support. I admit I am jealous."

With the cognition that everything can be dialectical, refute the above bias:

Zero dimension: There's no way to define the standard of ugliness.

Since everyone's aesthetic reference frame differs, the objective existence of "ugliness" cannot be proven. So "I am ugly" is a subjective thought—actually a self-bias.

One dimension: Why does such bias arise?

I think the causes are three: projection thinking, others' evaluations, and group identity. Projection thinking means seeing criticism of someone's appearance in media and projecting it onto oneself. Others' evaluations are face-to-face comments on appearance. Group identity means strong group connections in a certain aesthetic reference frame instilling specific aesthetics into individuals. Rebuttals: Projection thinking is a malignant analogical thinking that makes people see only "similarities" and not "differences." In a sense, those with projection thinking deny the diversity of appearance themselves. Others' evaluations can be joking or malicious. If you're sufficiently familiar with someone and know their personality, it's often joking or appropriate teasing to lighten the mood. Malicious means subjectively outputting one's own views to attack others—such people are often low-minded, morally low-dimensional; they should be avoided rather than causing self-negation. Group identity means people with similar aesthetics gather to seek identity and gain pleasure. This is actually a decision between group and individual, entirely depending on the firmness of one's self-cognition. If the group imposes aesthetics on the individual, it constrains the individual's right to choose aesthetics.

Two dimensions: How to remove self-bias?

Using my own reference frame, think reversely to find the bias at the other end:

"I am a very beautiful person. So beautiful people don't need to do better in other areas to gain recognition. Beautiful people need to dress up, because dressing up makes them even more beautiful—like a positive number plus another positive number yielding a larger positive number. That person is ugly, so they don't gain support. I admit I am arrogant."

In my reference frame, this looks like the thinking of deformed aesthetic groups. In reverse, my own bias against my appearance is also a kind of deformed aesthetics, just at the opposite end of the axis—six of one, half a dozen of the other. Recognizing this fact, I start to examine my inner self and suddenly feel relieved.

Three dimensions: How to find balance?

Undeniably, appearance has two sides like most attributes. "Beautiful" and "ugly" are on the same dimensional axis and can only be judged by subjective intuition. Roughly, "looking harmonious" is tied to "beautiful," "looking disharmonious" to "ugly." To find a suitable aesthetic balance point for yourself, ask three questions: Does appearance greatly affect the industry you will work in? Does it greatly affect your mate selection criteria? Does it raise your expectations of others?

If your industry relies purely on looks, then the industry is at the public group aesthetic end. If looks aren't needed, use your own reference frame to find other aspects of harmony. For example, professional designers have their own design rules, illustrators have their own color habits, creators have their own writing/painting style, programmers have their own module specifications, etc.

Mate selection criteria are more influenced by one's original family. Usually, liking someone filters their appearance, and fantasies in the mind raise unnecessary expectations. In fact, appearance's impact on relationships fades over time—it won't be a determining mate selection criterion. The truly determining attribute is attitude toward life.

Expectations of others depend on first impressions—a common psychology. I neither reject nor accept this psychology; it depends on specific cases. However, since those who conform to public taste are often adored and receive more kindness, it can be said that good-looking people are often more friendly and easy to get along with, less prone to negative emotions—an advantageous skill. It's okay to have slightly higher expectations for such people.

Four dimensions: Finding your own beautiful side?

If we equate "harmony" with "beauty," then finding your own beauty is equivalent to inducing a personal order from chaos. Start diverging from the word "beauty": courage, intellect, acting freely without transgressing boundaries, sky, forest, fleeting sunset, living toward death, cyan... The keywords I find by intuition may be my perception of "my own" beauty, or perhaps the group I hope to approach? I start to seriously think about this question.
