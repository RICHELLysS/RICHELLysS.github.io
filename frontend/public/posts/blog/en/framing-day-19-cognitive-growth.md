# Cognitive Growth

*Published on December 16, 2020*

I haven't written about cognitive science since the fifth Framing Day. After a long time, I'm picking up this topic again. Some of my views have changed after reading certain books. The change is that I now focus more on the appearance of cognition and avoid thinking too much about biological issues related to brain cognitive regions.

This time, I want to discuss how fixed mindset can become growth mindset. First, assume there are two kinds of creatures in the world: ordinary people and big shots (this classification isn't standard—just subjective). Ordinary people have a fixed mindset, meaning when facing something, their first internal question is: "Can I do this?" Big shots have a growth mindset, meaning their first internal question is: "How can I do this?" Now define two constants: human nature = lazy (0), anti-human nature = relatively not lazy (10). According to various data and surveys from cognitive scientists (I won't include the charts because I didn't find them—not rigorous, but the principle holds), people with fixed mindset tend to be bound to laziness. If they want to become growth mindset, they must experience suffering. In other words, ordinary people become big shots by suffering, and the process of suffering is the process of learning. More colloquially, as long as ordinary people overcome all kinds of self-negation like "I suck, I'm garbage" and force themselves to keep learning, they will become somewhat better and start enjoying the learning process. This is the core idea behind the saying "suffering is a blessing." In the first diagram, I believe that "trend" is the biggest difference between people. "Trend" is influenced by external factors: family background, IQ, EQ, appearance, etc. The so-called different starting points refer to different "trends." "Trend" indicates the difficulty of acquiring a growth mindset and the subsequent growth potential. It can also refer to belief (mentioned in the ninth Framing Day). In common saying, the more confident a person is, the more they can grow (no wonder I've always been garbage—stop scolding me). I can't express more analysis in words—just look at the diagram. The second diagram isn't drawn randomly. I remember seeing a curve somewhere but couldn't find it, so I drew it from memory. It roughly looks like that. In simple terms, this curve shows that when some ordinary people see big shots doing things, they start to be sour and picky, saying "I could do that" or "I already knew that." They are representatives of fixed mindset. Those with growth mindset or those in the trough of suffering tend to have admiration for big shots. And true big shots have admiration for everything, because they can always find something to learn from others. The condensed principle is: those who live by looking at others' strengths are themselves excellent. Those who understand will naturally understand. I don't know how to explain the principles and label meanings in detail—it's like the second diagram visually summarizes the first. A picture worth a thousand words.

Side note: If "trend" were to be translated into English, I think it's "potential," analogous to voltage in a circuit. The higher the voltage, the faster the system runs (not necessarily true in reality—just a mental framework connection).
