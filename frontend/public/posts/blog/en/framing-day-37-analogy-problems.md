# Analogy Problems

*Published on March 7, 2021*

I'm thinking about how to formulate imaginative learning. I raise some questions to organize my thoughts: imagination, metaphor, and analogy are roughly similar, so I'll tentatively define them as such. Based on this, I propose three examples:

1. Road analogized to circuit.
2. The relationship between the sun and planets analogized to atoms and electrons.
3. The motion of a pendulum analogized to a swing.

The commonality of the three examples: the analogizable part has "invariant" characteristics; the non-analogizable part has "variable" characteristics.

The "invariant" characteristics of roads and circuits: both can be abstracted as a model of nodes and channels. Based on this commonality, the power source can be analogized to a departure point, resistance to a narrow road, capacitance to a parking lot, inductance to a circular ramp. The "variable" characteristics: different scales, different specific values and external influences, different driving sources—in circuits, electron motion; in roads, vehicle and pedestrian motion.

Using the above definition of characteristics to analyze the second example: when a person learns the Rutherford atomic model, they picture several small circles around a large circle. When later learning planetary orbital motion, they also picture several small circles around a large circle. At this point, using analogical learning can reduce brain usage. How? Assume the "invariant" part is analogizable, then the brain only needs to remember the "variable" part to learn new knowledge. This method compresses knowledge and improves memory. In this example, the "invariant" part is the model structure of small circles orbiting a large circle. The "variable" part is the difference in properties between electrons and planets: in the atomic model, the number of electrons in a given orbital depends on the element's atomic number, and the forces between them can be affected by electromagnetism; in the planetary model, each orbit has only one major planet, and motion is affected by gravity. By analogizing this way, you don't need to take too many notes when learning (lazy people rejoice).

Moving to the third example, raising a new question: humans easily grasp the connection between a pendulum and a swing, so engineering experiments analogize from large models to small models. But computers do not easily understand this. If asked to recognize a swing, a computer gives various swings but cannot analogize to a pendulum—very rigid. So I start thinking: if a computer could define the "invariant" characteristics of things and recognize the "variable" characteristics, could it learn by analogy? Would learning efficiency be faster? If such an algorithm exists, I'll tentatively call it the "Imagination Algorithm." With the above approach, the next step is to think about how to describe "invariant" and "variable" in mathematical language.
