# Binary Thinking Modes

*Published on May 18, 2021*

I previously didn't know from which angle to divide "thinking modes" into two. After watching "Tenet," I suddenly had inspiration: categorize by causality.

First, build a thinking chain. We all know why quantum mechanics is incompatible with macroscopic physics: because experimental results show "effect" occurring before "cause" (I think this might be the inspiration for "Tenet"). Science has always built its system on the axioms "cause precedes effect" and "practice is the sole criterion of truth." This collides with the essential thought of mysticism, which typically believes all "effects" are predestined, thus deriving the function of predicting the future by intuition. Without arguing right or wrong, summarize: the core of science is "cause before effect"; the core of mysticism is "effect before cause." Their processes correspond to reasoning+practice and hypothesis+verification, respectively. Continuing to simplify, we can extract the concepts of "logic" and "intuition." Then we can subjectively divide thinking modes into "logical thinking mode" and "intuitive thinking mode."

Under the premise of focusing only on function, not on right/wrong or benefit, designing something can be based on logic or intuition. Whether something is excellent and powerful depends on which group can more skillfully manifest their thinking mode and solve problems efficiently. Design itself can be divided into "engineering" and "art." "Engineering" does not entirely correspond to "logic," nor does "art" entirely correspond to "intuition." Common cases: engineering-art design requires logic to break down and learn structures, and intuition to innovate—e.g., vehicle and building exteriors. Art-engineering requires intuition to learn structures and logic to innovate—e.g., theatrical effect and stage direction. There are also different combinations; different natures and no domain conflicts, so there is no hierarchy.

Typically, I think men think with logical thinking mode, and women with intuitive thinking mode, leading to interpretive divergences in various aspects. For example, in a common plot: something is lost. The male thinking loop: "I didn't pay attention at that time — too careless — I might lose it again next time." The female thinking mode: "I might lose it again next time — too careless — I didn't pay attention at that time." What does this small difference cause? Women first use intuition to see the outcome in their minds, easily generating emotions before calm thinking. Men first use logic to see the cause, easily engaging in calm thinking before producing emotions. If you can map this to everyday stories, you'll find it makes sense—sometimes the root cause of conflict outbreaks. Of course, creating stereotypes is not advisable. Here "male" and "female" only represent thinking modes. After all, everyone's psychological gender is fluid. According to the saying "powerful people are androgynous," it's easy to see that if one can possess both thinking modes and use them skillfully, they can become very powerful.

While building the above concepts, the 2048 model appeared in my mind (seems it wants to show its existence). Preliminary definition: the lines in the model represent "logic," the circles represent "intuition." This is a conjecture my intuition tells me. Next, I need to find verification tools.

Based on the causality approach, I found the statistical tool of causal inference: its essence is using data to infer the relationship between one element and another. Align "1" with statistical indicators, "0" with statistical verification. The purpose of setting statistical indicators is to achieve data predictability and as a decision-making reference model. The purpose of setting statistical verification is to use intervenable elements and other connectable elements to improve the credibility of statistical verification. Can this tool distinguish different users' thinking modes and build the 2048 model? That's the question I need to research next.
