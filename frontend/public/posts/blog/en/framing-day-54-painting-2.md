# Painting 2.0

*Published on June 23, 2021*

Lying on the sofa looking at the ceiling, the painting "The Creation of Adam" suddenly popped into my mind. I can't recall exactly when I saw it, but I remember the tour guide saying Michelangelo painted this fresco for four years, almost single-handedly completing a 600-square-meter ceiling masterpiece. As a child, I was deeply shaken. Then another scene emerged: I was sitting in a café, using my phone to Baidu Michelangelo's information and jotting it down. The reason: our English class had studied a text about da Vinci, and the teacher assigned us to collect information on other artists. Due to my impression of Michelangelo, I completed the assignment in the café using Baidu (why I remember this so clearly? Probably because the drink was terrible, making it a memory point).

What I remember most about "The Creation of Adam" is the tour guide mentioning that Michelangelo was gay and liked to paint all the male genitals, until one section was criticized by the Pope for being obscene, forcing him to add a cloth to each. Also, the church was so dark that you could only take blurry, smeared photos of the fresco; nothing was clear to the naked eye—terrible viewing experience.

Why did I think of "The Creation of Adam" while looking at my own ceiling? Because I remembered secretly drawing on my bedroom walls as a child, feeling highly superior that it rivaled the Dunhuang Mogao Caves frescoes, only to be harshly criticized? (Well, that might be part of it.) Actually, I analogized the distance between me and the ceiling to the distance between the two reaching hands in "The Creation of Adam." Their hands are very close, almost touching, but there's a gap. (My OCD really wants to fill that gap.) In an instant, I quickly diverged many analogies: the distance between brain and body in the painting, between ideal and reality, between dream and consciousness, between elephant and mouse, between ceiling and me... The atmosphere became philosophical. I also recalled that in his later years, Michelangelo's eyes were nearly blinded by years of dripping paint, yet he still incredibly completed his great work, dying shortly after. This is quite similar to Beethoven's late creative state. It also echoes the name "The Creation" — he truly created a century of his own. The ultimate form of a creator, perhaps. What I find sad is that the religious meaning of "The Creation" is far more famous than its creator. People love to adore the gods in the painting, interpret the religious symbolic roles of different figures, focus on the economic benefits the painting brings to local industries, but rarely show reverence to the creator. Perhaps they only seek the "divinity" the creator manifested through the painting, mapping it onto their imagined deities, unwilling to believe that mortal bodies can burst forth infinite potential. From a certain angle, like moths flying toward light and avoiding darkness, they don't realize they are actually yearning for someone else's shadow (strange metaphor added). Perhaps this too can be analogized to the insurmountable gap in "The Creation" — so close, yet so far.
