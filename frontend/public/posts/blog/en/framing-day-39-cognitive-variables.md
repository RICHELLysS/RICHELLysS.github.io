# Cognitive Variables

*Published on March 10, 2021*

Before defining variables related to "cognition," following the scientific principle of "bold hypothesis, careful verification," I first assume that the analogy method I proposed in the 37th framing day is feasible. Here I will take some "invariant" attributes that I think can be analogized from other fields to apply to defining "cognition."

The analogy reference for the first figure comes from the LC circuit oscillation formula and the electromagnetic wave propagation speed formula. I assume the cognitive constant y can be analogized to the form of the above two formulas. As shown in Figure 1, a and b are anxiety index and cognitive efficiency, respectively. They are inversely proportional, meaning the more anxious, the lower the cognitive efficiency. The cognitive constant determines the cognitive level at a given moment, and its relationship with a and b is temporarily expressed using the analogized formula.

In the 21st framing day, I mentioned that ordinary people's cognition at birth is not much different; differences arise only due to material and environmental factors. Therefore, I believe the anxiety value a changes with external interference, defined as the degree of information disorder. As shown in Figure 2, external information is divided into two types: accurate and chaotic. Taking sound waves as an example: when an auditory learner listens to a lecture, the teacher's content is relatively accurate (wave frequency one). At the same time, other wave frequencies come from outside—construction, classmates' discussions, book flipping, etc. Information becomes chaotic, anxiety unconsciously rises, and cognitive efficiency for knowledge decreases. The same applies to visual and kinesthetic learners.

For the breadth and depth of cognition, I analogize using the formula of light wave intensity decreasing with distance. In the 27th framing day, I mentioned the concept of abstract dimensions. Analogously, cognition also has dimensions. As shown in Figure 3, this concept is somewhat abstract. Using a three-dimensional luminous object as a metaphor: at the object's surface, light intensity is highest, area is smallest. The farther away, the lower the intensity, the larger the area. Assuming cognition also has a referable object: increasing depth means approaching the object; increasing breadth means moving away from the object. This may to some extent explain the cognitive difference between scientific researchers and artists.

Cognitive delay is often encountered in learning. I think its level of wonder is on par with quantum mechanics. As shown in Figure 4, the most common example: one day you rack your brain and can't figure out how to write a program, but the next day you inexplicably have an idea and write it out. Or one day you want to build with Lego but face a pile of pieces without a clue, then after sleeping, you can immediately build something. This cognitive delay, I think, is actually the mind jumping from a lower dimension to a higher dimension, requiring only a transition period—similar to the cooldown bar of a skill in a game.

Because cognition differs between people, cognitive bias is inevitable. In the 23rd framing day, I mentioned the concept of "the tip of the iceberg seen." Here I will make it more abstract through analogy. As shown in Figure 5, first propose an unproven conclusion: low-dimensional cognition leads to underestimation of others; high-dimensional cognition leads to overestimation of others. We know that relatively speaking, the lower dimension is actually a projection of the higher dimension. Thus, without the influence of labels, low-dimensional cognition can only see the projection of the higher dimension, while the higher dimension treats the lower dimension as higher. This is somewhat similar to Plato's cave theory.

Metaphor is a very useful cognitive tool. Its essence is to describe things that are originally imperceptible as perceptible. As shown in Figure 6, humans are lazy creatures, liking intuitive and easy-to-understand things and getting used to staying in the comfort zone. Thus, metaphor is very important. Some abstract and complex things can be simplified through metaphor for rapid acceptance. For example, analogize the universe to a jianbing (Chinese crepe). Batter slapped on the iron plate = the Big Bang. Spreading it out = universe expansion. Sprinkling scallions on the spread-out batter = birth of stars. Stars have bean paste, chili sauce, cowpeas, lettuce, ham slices... (getting more absurd). Poking a hole in the rolled-up jianbing = a wormhole. Doesn't this make astronomy full of human touch (not really).
