# Building the 2048 Model

*Published on April 16, 2021*

Based on the previous 47 framing days, I attempt to construct the 2048 model using existing ideas. This construction system does not conform to any conventional logic; it is proposed solely based on my own thinking style and intuitive feelings.

First, as shown in Figure 1, in my defined cognitive system, the analogical dimensions of all things do not exceed 4. According to the understanding of cognition from the first framing day, define a thing as abstracting a long grid containing "what," "why," "how," and "where positioned." Treat this grid as a "unit," represented by "0." Here "0" differs from the mathematical concept of 0; it represents a divergence point with two possible divergence directions: "-1" and "1," representing the two sides of things.

As shown in Figure 2, start diverging. I imagine all things in my perception that favor "bright" and favor "dark," then subdivide them, entirely by feeling. "Feeling" is hard to define; everyone's "feeling" differs—probably the brain's unique jumping mechanism. In the 36th framing day, I mentioned my thoughts on color intuition. This is because my classification of things is based either on numbers or on colors. How specifically? For example, I classify mathematics as "blue category," sports as "cyan category," literature as "orange category," economics as "yellow category"—very subjective. Most of the time, thinking of a thing associates with a corresponding color, whether abstract or concrete. Since I don't know what characteristic I'm using for classification, I'll temporarily represent this characteristic as "A." In the two-division stage, A can be represented by (x, y), where x is white, y is black, similar to grayscale in color theory, analogizable to the good and bad sides of things. In the four-division stage, add a dimension z, representing the development direction of things—this stage is cognitive structure. The final eight-division stage is the implementation stage, adding the time variable t, classifying the essence of things into a color according to the characteristics of "A."

As shown in Figure 3, the entire process of classification, definition, and implementation involves all things that I denote as region(A), abbreviated R(A). The definition of R(A) itself determines its classifiability. For the part outside R(A), i.e., R(A'), the undefined part, I have two hypotheses about their relationship: 1) R(A) and R(A') are at opposite ends of a number line, in opposing relation; 2) Using the defined R(A) as the center, diverge to eight endpoints, R(A) chooses one endpoint direction as reference, and the remaining endpoints are R(A'). Which hypothesis better fits the 2048 rule is where I need to continue thinking.

Combining the 47th framing day: people see things from different angles, so the shape of cognition differs. I assume a thing can be abstracted as a circle, viewable from countless angles depending on the degree of subdivision. Then treat R(A) itself as a divergence point, rethink the problems corresponding to different dimensions, as shown in Figures 4 and 5. Theoretically, this forms a nested (matryoshka) structure.

This model does not conform to any modern logical system. To abstract it into a mathematical model requires clearer definition of its elements. It does not belong to any philosophical "-ism," because I cannot clearly understand many definitions of concepts in philosophy. If something cannot be simply and intuitively represented by drawing, I have difficulty imagining its shape. So I can only subjectively define it using my own model. I don't know where it can be applied. But whenever I face anything or think about a problem, such a model appears in my mind, helping me quickly understand knowledge. Existence is meaningful. Perhaps by gradually making it concrete, it could become a good tool in some field.
