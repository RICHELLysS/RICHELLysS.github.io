# Media

*Published on May 26, 2020*

Random thoughts on media:

The internet is a parallel line in this game world, carrying the spiritual world. When the material world is unsatisfied or oversaturated, people tend to seek pleasure in the spiritual world. But the more people join, quantitative change leads to qualitative change. I remember an episode of SpongeBob SquarePants satirizing online platforms. Initially, a blackboard in the restaurant was for customers to give opinions and communicate. Gradually, as more people joined, opinions diverged, and people started cursing each other. Since opinions were anonymous, no matter how harsh the words, there were no consequences. Mr. Krabs smelled business opportunities, deliberately created controversial topics, and got more people involved in the arguments. Finally, the board was smashed, everyone's names were exposed, and they fled.

Something like that—the details are fuzzy, but that episode left a deep impression on me before I even had internet access. Back to the topic: what is the role of media? Initially, just reporting news. But if it were that pure, there would be no profit. Now, with more and more users, media primarily have three purposes:

1. Make money. Whether fake or real news, as long as it makes money, it's good news. Usually ads and promotions.

2. Gain attention. Same principle. As long as it attracts attention—regardless of distorting facts or intentionally creating provocative headlines—attention equals market share.

3. Stir up trends to promote the flow of attention markets. Because the internet is so fast-food now, everyone goes online for fun or to find a sense of belonging in so-called similar groups. Few people seriously sort out causes and effects or think independently. So many netizens make up their minds based solely on headlines.

These three purposes magnify one of the internet's flaws: labeling.

Everywhere online you see labels: Northeasterners, feminists, fan circles, elementary schoolers, little pinkos (can't think of more examples). These labels represent certain groups with common characteristics. There's no strict definition—it's very subjective. One person cannot represent a group, but once labeled, they are taken as representing a group. As long as a group exists, it has extremes: extremely rational and extremely irrational people at opposite ends of a spectrum. In simple terms, there are good and bad. You see two groups constantly arguing, each representing the irrational end of their group. For example, feminists: the extremes of support and opposition are usually "women should have all the rights they deserve" vs. "women are incapable and just talk." Both extremes deviate from the true goal of equal human rights. Another example, fan circles: the opposing sides are "unconditionally support one person" vs. "don't pollute the environment with fandoms." What really needs to be addressed is the group that lacks manners and goes everywhere off-topic, not blind support or total prohibition. Another example, patriotism (a bit sensitive): the conflict is clearly between mindlessly praising the country and mindlessly bashing it. The proper cognitive state is to recognize that patriotism is a form of ideology while weighing its pros and cons for people and groups.

I wrote all this because I saw people on some garbage software (personal opinion) cursing without understanding the facts, having been led by the headline without clicking into the article to sort things out. Since a chain of people were led by the same headline, it's an interesting phenomenon.

Sometimes I think about why we always see negative words online. I conclude it might be survivorship bias: excellent people rarely express opinions easily, so what we see online are only the words of "certain people."

Random conclusion: group effects are worth studying. Today I mentioned group polarization and labeling. With a few more definitions of properties, maybe a model can be built.

I recall a quote from Lu Xun: "Beasts always walk alone; only cattle and sheep flock together." Quite sharp, very enlightening. Although a bit harsh, the logic is easy to understand.
