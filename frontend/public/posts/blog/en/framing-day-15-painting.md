# Painting

*Published on June 1, 2020*

I seem to have some connection with painting. When I was a child, I went to the library not to read literature, but to read comics. For me, images are easier to understand than text. Sometimes I'd browse artist appreciation books. What impressed me most was Van Gogh's sunflowers in a green vase, which stuck in my mind. Later, in art class, I painted red lilies and Bauhinia flowers in a white vase. I also remember taking art classes at the children's palace, learning about many artists. Although someone's prejudice brainwashed me into thinking artists only become famous after death and don't make money while alive, I never wanted to make big money—just a small house to sleep, eat, and paint. Back then, I thought I'd grow out of such naive thoughts, but even now, this lifestyle is my ideal. Today is Children's Day, and at eighteen, I'm still this naive.

Everyone's understanding of a painting is subjective. Many similar subjective opinions combined become objective—that's statistics. For me, a good painting conveys the thought or emotion it intends to express. "Expression" is the core of art forms, whether literature, film, painting, or sculpture. They all aim to convey something to the world, though each person's subjective interpretation differs.

As my biggest hobby, while collecting material, I saw familiar artists again, so I want to record my subjective evaluations and memorable works. Maybe this will become a series?

## 1 Van Gogh's "Sunflowers"

Van Gogh painted many sunflowers, but the one I remember is a green vase with bright, vivid sunflowers (1888—easy to remember because of all the eights). In my mind, those sunflowers are constantly spinning, perhaps related to Van Gogh's swirling brushwork. Van Gogh's emotions are very vivid, somewhat rugged, not delicate but presenting raw beauty directly. You feel he is a very emotional person, yet unhurriedly sketching the scenery he passes. I remember watching "Loving Vincent"—I forgot the plot entirely, was captivated by the visuals, and cried leaving the theater because it was so beautiful. I instantly fell in love with Van Gogh's world.

## 2 Monet's "Woman with a Parasol"

If Van Gogh's emotions are vivid, making you unconsciously feel his rich feelings and desire to express, then Monet is hazy and restrained, painting what he sees. Monet's paintings have an unreal quality, like déjà vu in a dream, on the boundary between reality and fantasy, just right—neither too clear nor too blurry. That's probably the charm of Impressionism. I love many of Monet's paintings, but "Woman with a Parasol" is most memorable: classic blue, white, and gold colors; a girl in a field looking back, as if all details are forgotten, only an impression remains. That's the perfect visual expression (personal preference).
