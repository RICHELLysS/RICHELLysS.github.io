# Empathy Zero

*Published on January 14, 2020*

Today is Tuesday. I lost contact with my limbic system again, causing my empathy to drop to zero. Everyone has a different perspective, but people often overlook this, requiring constant communication to connect to the other's channel. After experiencing many conscious, unconscious, and belated cross-server chats, I understood the difference between common knowledge and public knowledge. Common knowledge is known by a group but not known whether others know it; public knowledge is known by a group and known that others know it. The best example is the Emperor's New Clothes: everyone knows the emperor is naked, but doesn't know if others know—that's common knowledge. When a child shouts it out, everyone realizes they're not alone—that becomes public knowledge. These two concepts can be broken down into a model of interpersonal communication. When two people communicate, say A and B, one usually talks more (assume A), and B receives more information. If their common knowledge is equal, B will understand A better because he knows which knowledge is public. If their common knowledge is unequal, disjoint public knowledge appears. If neither realizes this (i.e., not carefully receiving the other's information), cross-server chatting occurs, leading to limbic system insecurity and embarrassment (this concept might analyze conversational EQ). My current solution is to record unknown information from the conversation, find similarities in past experiences, generate equal common information, and then obtain unilateral public information to better judge how to communicate. This method is used when the limbic system is on strike. If the limbic system is working, I can directly use induced emotions to drive communication.
