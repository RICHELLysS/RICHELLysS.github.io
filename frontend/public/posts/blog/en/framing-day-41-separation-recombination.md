# Separation and Recombination Modules

*Published on March 14, 2021*

Before discussing these two concepts, first describe an abstract model, as shown in the figure. Suppose we start with a complete square. How can it be cut into any other shape? You might think this is a math or topology problem, but actually it's about imagination. If I say a square can become any shape, conventional thinking says it's impossible, but upon reflection, following the approach of modern computer displays, it's feasible. You can cut a square into many small squares, then reassemble them into different shapes. The figure divides the square into 25 small squares, then rotates the corner squares by 22.5 degrees, forming a rhombus-like shape. The finer the cutting, the higher the variability. If we number each small square and record "variable" and "invariant" numbers, the recombination efficiency of the blocks increases (this is just a hypothesis; I haven't found a way to abstract it into mathematics).

Stepping back from abstraction, start diverging with imagination. We can analogize the square to any concept. Cutting and subdividing the square is the process of "task separation." Each small square can be seen as a module, then recombined (is this too abstract?).

The term "task separation" is borrowed from psychology. It originally means drawing psychological boundaries. The closer the relationship, the harder it is to draw boundaries. For example, in a couple, both think the other should be responsible for their emotions. Task separation means dividing boundaries: your own affairs are your own responsibility. This concept applies even more to parent-child relationships. Parents have a bad habit: "Because you didn't satisfy me with what you did, I am angry, so you should be responsible for my emotions." Sometimes this escalates to personal attacks, which is illogical. In such situations, task separation is very important. With sufficient psychological construction, you can lecture your parents: "You need to adjust your own emotions; I am not responsible for them. Similarly, my emotions are my own business; you cannot interfere. This is called task separation."

Slightly off-track. This concept can be analogized to many fields, simplifying and organizing a system (though still a theoretical hypothesis). For example, think: "How to write a 2048 mini-game? And how to analogize from 2048 to Snake and Tetris?" Before writing, use imagination to abstract the game into a large square in your mind, then logically cut it into several small blocks, while reassembling, considering "variable" and "invariant" modules by analogy.

In the simplest case without considering the interface, induce the three games: the "invariant" aspects: each grid has x and y coordinates, operated by four keys (up, down, left, right / WSAD), need to generate a "map," both have "score" and "status" variables, both have a "random generation" module. The "variable" aspects: only the "scoring mechanism," "random generation method," and map size. We can boldly say that as long as different functions are correctly defined, the three games can exchange many modules.

Continuing to analogize on a larger scale: why do people group "math, physics, chemistry" and "history, geography, biology" together, but rarely say "literature, art, music" as a similar group? Probably because the disciplines in the former groups have many "analogizable" characteristics. Mathematics is highly logical, abstracted from physics and also assisting physics development. Chemistry is a subdivision of physics, also inheriting mathematics and assisting more microscopic physics. "History, geography, biology" gradually approach the humanities, becoming less abstract, more about measuring objective existence. "Literature, art, music" become a kind of "concrete" recombination, another layer of abstraction (how to express this more clearly? If the original large square is "math, physics, chemistry," then "literature, art, music" is the reassembled rhombus-like shape, also an abstract object to be cut, yielding finer graphics less easily defined by logic).

Stephen Hawking said the next era is not the era of biology or computers, but the era of complexity. Data and information grow exponentially. Complex systems always need simplification methods. I think the more abstract something is, the more simplified it is—meaning a method to simplify a system is to make it abstract. If we determine the "invariant" in a system and only operate on the "variable" parts, could that be a way to turn complexity into simplicity? (There it is, the question monster.)
