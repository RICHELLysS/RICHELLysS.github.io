# Combining Montage and Symbolism

*Published on March 31, 2021*

I've become fascinated with montage as an expression technique. However, many sources have vague definitions of this technique, only providing examples. So I'll try to induce it using my own rules.

First, define montage: a technique that uses fragmented, non-continuous footage to achieve the purpose of expressing some change. If I abstract montage, I can find its shadow in fields other than film, including literature and art—e.g., "Miracles of the Namiya General Store," "Up." When combined with symbolism, it produces wonderful chemical reactions.

Preliminary definition of the abstract concept of montage: a symbolic element in a space changes over time to symbolize a meaning, using fragmented text or shots to tell this change. That is, montage combined with symbolism is an advanced technique for describing change.

Zero dimension: What is the symbolic element?

The symbolic element depends on the theme the screenwriter wants to express. Any analogizable thing can be chosen for divergence. For example, an apple—use imagination to give it meanings: completeness, heart, life, bloodline, beauty, sweetness, etc.

One dimension: Who is the carrier of the symbolic element?

The carrier is not limited to characters; it can be anything possessing two attributes: one is direction, the other is tendency. These two attributes can be analogized to electromagnetism or to kinetic/potential energy (off-track). The carrier's identity is determined by the screenwriter, and identity usually determines direction and tendency to some extent, thus direction and tendency establish certain inevitable connections with the symbolic element.

Two dimensions: How does the symbolic element change?

Continuing with the apple example: an apple can have many changes, spatial or temporal. Within a story system, to symbolize something, many changes can derive: falling (character's status change), bitten (character's personality defect), rotting (character走向 extremes and destruction), etc.

Three dimensions: Interaction between symbolic element and carrier?

If the carrier's shaping is sufficiently three-dimensional, the symbolic element becomes a good booster, making events inevitable in a certain space. This tests the creator's skill and requires specific case analysis.

Four dimensions: Impact on plot direction.

A fragment can be text or a shot, and the change expressed within it is closely linked to the symbolic element. Here I use a linked list as an analogy. Take two plot directions, assuming the ending is either Bad Ending or Happy Ending. The entire film uses montage. Now imagine a positive cosine wave ranging from 0 to 180 degrees. Use 1 to represent "completeness," and the carrier's fate continuously goes downward. You can imagine how glamorous and abundant the character is at the beginning, then gradually becomes down and out, finally having nothing (-1). From 1 to -1 results in a Bad Ending. I think of Scarlett in "Gone with the Wind" (though not perfectly fitting). You'll find that symbolism allows you to use a curve in your mind to represent a character's fate. If you successfully fit the curve to the character, the following concepts become easier to understand. Now imagine a negative cosine wave, also ranging 0-180 degrees. Can you relate it to any character's trajectory? I think of Elizabeth's emotional arc in "Pride and Prejudice": from prejudice against Mr. Darcy (-1) to admiration (1). From -1 to 1 results in a Happy Ending. Insert fragments on the curve to highlight symbolic elements, using montage to tell the story. I think this can increase a work's artistry (though it reduces realism and favors stream of consciousness). The approach is to bind a symbolic thing to a character. For Scarlett, I think she is like a flower, and the film has a pastoral field. So each time her fate changes, use montage to insert a shot of a flower changing in the field (but not stealing the character's expression): vibrant, bee pollinating, withering, fading—forming the symbol of the character's fate.

Using the induced rules above, analyze two works I like to further explain the clever combination of montage and symbolism.

"Millennium Actress" by Satoshi Kon

From my perspective, this is a work that takes the combination of montage and symbolism to the extreme. Only animation can achieve this effect. It's easy to notice that the symbolic element is the key the female protagonist wears. The key initially symbolizes "love," but by the end, it symbolizes "obsession." The female protagonist's direction and tendency are also closely related to "love" and "obsession." The transition between the two uses montage, ignoring time and space, serving symbolism primarily, interweaving the direction of inside and outside the story, creating a powerful stream-of-consciousness atmosphere. Its purpose is not to describe dramatic conflict but to describe the character's inner changes. The symbolism lies in "the pursuit drives the character's direction; things have no result, but the tendency of the pursuit itself achieves a person's life." In the latter part of the anime, where montage quickly switches scenes, you can very clearly feel the symbolic meaning expressed.

"Cloud Atlas" by Tom Tykwer

I originally thought David Mitchell's original work was impossible to film, but the movie refreshed my understanding. It was also the first time I encountered the fragment-intercut technique (which in my view is montage). The symbolic elements in the film are the birthmarks on the six protagonists, or rather, the six protagonists themselves are symbolic elements. The change described by montage is "fate"—fate as "breaking through some predicament, confronting forces, seeking truth." Six timelines are interwoven, telling the same symbolic story: confronting racial discrimination, confronting homophobia, confronting the silencing of speech, confronting the plight of the elderly, confronting the fate of clones, confronting ancient civilization. Its brilliance lies in montage interleaving and combining the beginnings and climaxes of the six stories, like a patchwork painting, or like six waves overlapping to perform a grand symphony—highly consistent with the "Cloud Atlas Sextet" in the film. The combination of montage and symbolism takes this consistency to another level.
