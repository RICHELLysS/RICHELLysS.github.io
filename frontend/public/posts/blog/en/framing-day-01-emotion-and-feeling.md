# Emotion and Feeling

*Published on December 18, 2019*

What is the occasion for humans to generate emotions? It’s like telling you y and asking you to find x. Machines have no emotions, meaning one of the differences between humans and machines is the transmissibility of feelings. Feelings are like electromagnetic induction: one person's emotional fluctuation can affect another, but the induced feeling is different from the original. This rough conjecture leads to a conclusion: "Human feelings are not the same, but they can induce each other." Due to the difficulty of sample collection and the shallow research on brainwaves, it's impossible to verify this hypothesis through large-scale experiments. To give an example: when your friend complains about something, if you haven't had a similar experience, you may not understand her anger and might instead become irritated. When you share joy with others, they may politely accept it and try to adjust to a similar joyful emotion, but if they can't accept it, it may turn into embarrassment.

Thinking this way, it's very difficult to evoke emotional resonance in many people. Even if reason analyzes the causes of emotions, it only knows the characteristics of certain emotional expressions. If only we could find a specific expression between emotions—perhaps everyone has an induction coefficient, and empathy is proportional to that coefficient, like a friction coefficient. The larger the coefficient, the smaller the difference between the induced emotion and the input emotion. Or there might be a deviation value, representing the angle between the induced emotion and the input emotion—for example, the deviation between happiness and sadness might be 180 degrees.

But thinking so much without being able to verify and derive an expression for this inductive relationship is just idle speculation.
