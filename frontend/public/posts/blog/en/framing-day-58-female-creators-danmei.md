# Female Creators and Danmei Works

*Published on August 1, 2021*

An interesting phenomenon: most authors who write and produce danmei works are female (excluding those catering to the gay male market for quick money), especially in written fiction. I want to discuss this topic because my enlightenment about love came from danmei novels. For some novels, I can project myself into one of the characters rather than observing from the outside to "ship" the CP. If defined by fandom terms, I am a book fan. For dramas, if the first episode doesn't interest me, I go directly to recaps. Because the book is the creator's original expression, without information gaps or bias angles interfering, and I can better feel the characters' emotions and motivations.

What I want to express next may be a bit sensitive. To make my view less subjective and emotional, I'll first point out some existing phenomena: there are far more female than male authors writing danmei. When female authors write danmei, the subject matter often spans wider and character development is fuller (military/political, suspense, workplace—not limited to family). Descriptions of sex in danmei are often more delicate and serve the expression and identification of the relationship. In my view, these female writers project their desired images onto the male characters in their books. Common tropes in web fiction—like overbearing CEO, Mary Sue, villain's comeback—also appear in danmei. The difference: the trope plays out between two males. Their public social identities are the same, and their gender power is equal. So when conflict arises, the concept of gender difference is weakened, and scenes of one bullying the other are rare. Such gender imagination becomes a vehicle for authors to express love, presenting a pattern where both parties jointly participate in social affairs, jointly compete for resources, and jointly contribute. From this perspective, danmei products are essentially the projection of women constrained by social images onto their ideal social relationships.

When capital enters the danmei market, product alienation triggers a series of butterfly effects. Some male capitalists develop a misunderstanding: female audiences just like watching two handsome men bicker, or female audiences just like mindless sweet romance. They often overlook the creators' logic. Consequently, the market floods with subjective "industrial saccharin" romance dramas and various borderline gay products. But from a macro creator perspective, a good work should present changes in relationship structures within the era, break through gender images defined by outdated concepts in current society, and through presenting different perspectives, enable more groups to empathize with different social roles—especially perspectives with women as the subject of power (and I privately hope screenwriters will communicate more with excellent female authors). If capital controllers only extract and copy one-sided labels of a certain type of work, the resulting products are essentially the dregs of the male gaze as their core.
